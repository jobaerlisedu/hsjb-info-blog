Types of Area Networks - LAN, MAN, and WAN

Networking January 02, 2026 Jobaer Hossain

Introduction

The Network allows computers to connect and communicate with different computers via any medium. LAN, MAN, and WAN are the three major types of networks designed to operate over the area they cover.

Personal Area Network (PAN)

A PAN is a short-range network that connects personal devices like smartphones, tablets, and computers. It typically covers less than 10 meters and usually uses wireless technologies such as Bluetooth.

  • Advantages:
    • Easy communication between personal devices in close proximity.
    • Uses wireless technology, eliminating wires.
    • Energy efficient.
  • Disadvantages:
    • Limited coverage area.
    • Limited bandwidth.
    • May experience interference.

Local Area Network (LAN)

A LAN connects computers and devices within a small area like a home, office, school, or hospital. It usually uses switches, routers, and private IP addresses.

  • Coverage: Up to 2 km
  • Speed: 100–1000 Mbps
  • Medium: Mostly wired (Ethernet), wireless possible
  • Example: Multiplayer game in the same room
  • Advantages:
    • Fast data transfer and high-speed.
    • Easy to set up and manage.
    • Share peripherals like printers.
  • Disadvantages:
    • Limited geographical coverage.
    • Limited scalability.
    • May experience congestion.

Metropolitan Area Network (MAN)

A MAN spans 5–50 km, covering a city or nearby cities. It connects multiple LANs and can act as an ISP.

  • Advantages:
    • High-speed over larger area than LAN.
    • Can serve as ISP.
    • Higher data rates in some cases.
  • Disadvantages:
    • Expensive to set up and maintain.
    • Congestion possible.
    • Limited fault tolerance and security.

Wide Area Network (WAN)

A WAN covers large geographical areas (above 50 km), connecting multiple LANs via telephone lines, radio waves, or satellites. The Internet is the largest WAN.

  • Advantages:
    • Covers large areas and remote locations.
    • Provides Internet connectivity.
    • Remote resource access.
  • Disadvantages:
    • Expensive.
    • Slower than LAN/MAN.
    • Lower fault tolerance and security.

Comparison of LAN, MAN, and WAN

Feature LAN MAN WAN
Coverage Up to 2 km 5–50 km Above 50 km
Speed 100–1000 Mbps High-speed (Mbps) High but slower than LAN/MAN
Medium Mostly wired Varies (fiber, leased lines) Telephone, radio, satellites
Cost Inexpensive Costly Expensive
Maintenance Easy Complex Difficult
Examples Home, office City-wide Internet

Key Differences

  • Coverage Area: LAN (small), MAN (city), WAN (large/global)
  • Speed: LAN highest, then MAN, WAN slowest
  • Cost & Complexity: LAN cheapest/easiest, WAN most expensive/complex
  • Use Cases: LAN local sharing, MAN city connectivity, WAN global
